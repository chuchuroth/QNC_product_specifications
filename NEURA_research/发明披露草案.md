# 发明披露草案——具备功能安全认证的高载荷自主热插拔连接机构

## 目的

本文件旨在作为一个具体的起点，可直接交给：

* 机械工程师 / 功能安全工程师（评估工程可行性）
* 专利律师（Patentanwalt）（进行新颖性检索、FTO（自由实施）分析以及专利撰写）

需要强调的是：

**这只是基于初步排除式检索（knock-out scan）形成的工作草案，并不是完整的新颖性检索，也不构成法律意见。**

文中涉及的各项单独技术特征本身，在现有技术中大多都已存在；本草案主张的创造性在于：

> **将这些技术特征以一种特定方式组合，并应用于“能够自主更换承重运动/肢体模块、且在人类附近工作的机器人”这一应用场景。**

# 一、拟解决的技术空白

目前存在两个彼此相邻、且专利布局十分密集的技术领域，

但是它们之间的交集仍然没有被覆盖。

### （1）自重构模块机器人（SRMR）连接器

例如：

* US 7,850,388
* US 8,234,950（USC SuperBot）

它们具有：

* 自主连接
* 无公母（genderless）设计
* 能够传递结构载荷、电力和通信

但是：

* 承载能力较小（实验室级模块，仅几十牛到几百牛、几十牛·米）
* **没有围绕功能安全联锁（functional safety interlock）设计。**

---

### （2）工业工具快换器

例如：

* US 4,551,903
* ATI 快换器
* DE 3,705,123
* US 8,500,132
* US 10,047,908

它们具有：

* 很高的承载能力；
* 机械、电力、流体接口同时连接；
* 多采用气动或人工操作。

但是：

* 面向固定机械臂末端工具；
* 并非机器人自主重构；
* 更不是用于机器人自身运动模块（locomotion module）的更换；
* 也没有围绕承重肢体所需的 **E-stop/STO 安全连续性** 进行设计。

---

### 本发明瞄准的技术空白

一种同时满足以下条件的连接机构：

* 高结构载荷（high structural load）
* 自主热插拔（autonomous hot-swap）
* 实时数据通信
* 可认证（certifiable）的功能安全联锁

适用于：

> **机器人依靠该模块站立、行走，甚至载人的场景。**

# 二、候选机构——区别于现有技术的关键特征

作者认为：

真正的创新核心，

并不是七项特征中的某一项，

而是：

> **A/B（安全承载锁紧）+ C（安全顺序连接）+ D/E（安全验证后才允许承载）**

这一组合。

---

## A. 载荷自增强、默认锁定、主动解锁的结构锁

结构载荷经过楔块/凸轮机构。

其几何关系设计为：

> **外部载荷越大，锁紧力越大。**

即：

具有自增强（self-energizing）特性。

系统默认状态：

* 机械锁定；
* 只有主动施加动力才允许解锁。

因此：

即使供电、供气全部丢失，

连接也不会松开，

反而会因为载荷增加而锁得更紧。

区别于：

ATI 等依靠气压保持锁定、压力降低可能释放的结构。 

---

## B. 双重独立锁紧机构

采用两个互相独立的锁扣，

共同承担载荷。

即使其中一个失效，

整个模块也不会掉落。

实现：

> **单故障容忍（single-fault tolerance）。**

区别于：

现有工具快换器以及 SRMR 大多只有单一锁紧机构。 

---

## C. 分阶段接通/断开顺序

整个连接过程严格按顺序进行：

1. 建立机械结构连接；
2. 验证机械锁定；
3. 建立双通道安全回路（E-stop/STO）；
4. 接通电源；
5. 建立数据通信。

拆卸时则完全反向：

首先停止驱动，

最后才释放机械锁。

区别于：

US 4,551,903 等现有方案，

它们都是：

> **机械、电源、流体同时连接。** 

---

## D. 双通道、不同原理的锁定验证

采用两种不同物理原理的传感器：

共同验证锁定状态。

机器人安全控制器只有在：

两个传感器一致确认锁定后，

才允许驱动。

目标达到：

Category 3 / PL d（或 SIL2）等级。

区别于：

ATI 仅采用普通接近传感器，

而不是安全认证双通道验证。 

---

## E. 与模块身份绑定的安全准入机制

机器人在承载之前，

读取模块电子身份（ID）及能力描述。

例如：

* 最大承载能力
* 安全等级
* 所需安全回路

如果：

* 承载等级不足；
* 安全验证失败；

机器人直接拒绝：

* 加载重量；
* 启动执行器。

作者特别说明：

这一思想可以与你提出的 **VMM（Virtual Module Manifest）** 结合，

但这里：

> **它不是简单描述信息，而是真正参与安全决策。** 

---

## F. 两阶段接口

第一阶段：

大容差柔性捕获（large area-of-acceptance compliant capture）

便于机器人自主对接。

第二阶段：

施加预紧，

形成：

高刚度、

无间隙、

能够承受运动载荷的连接。

结合了：

SRMR 的易对接，

以及工业工具快换器的高刚度。 

---

## G. 防反驱锁定

锁紧机构具有：

单向自锁几何结构。

机器人行走时：

长期振动、

周期冲击，

都不会逐渐把锁松开。

只有主动供能，

才能解锁。

# PART 1 — FREEDOM-TO-OPERATE ANALYSIS

## 1.1 Invention concept assessed

A robot (mobile home/service robot or robot arm) that, upon sensing that a **protected subject** (e.g., a child or pet) is approaching or exposed to an **environmental hazard** (stairs/drop-off, water/pool, hot surface, road, sharp implement, another moving machine), **deploys an inflatable barrier** — carried as a swappable end-effector on a standardized coupling — that rapidly expands from a compact stowed state into a **soft, compliant boundary extending beyond the robot's own footprint**, interposed between the subject and the hazard, and then retracts/deflates once the hazard is cleared.

## 1.2 Search scope and method

Public patent and technical literature scanned across these clusters: (a) robots that position a barrier between a person and a hazard; (b) autonomous "guardian" systems that detect risk to children/around water/fire and deploy interventions; (c) inflatable protective devices that deploy on a sensed event (wearable fall airbags; vehicle/cobot collision airbags); (d) inflatable/soft robot end-effectors and structures; (e) static child barriers/gates and robot guarding; (f) robot-confinement/virtual-barrier art. This is a **preliminary knock-out scan**, not an exhaustive clearance search — in particular, non-English (CN, JP, DE, KR) art and the **patent-family legal status / jurisdictional coverage** of the key references were **not** verified and are the single most important next step.

## 1.3 Landscape overview

The relevant art splits into three tiers by closeness to the concept.

**Tier 1 — Direct concept overlap (primary FTO risk).** Two references read on the *function* "a robot/system interposes a protective barrier or protective action between a person (including a child) and a hazard." These are the references that can block the broad concept regardless of inflation.

**Tier 2 — Inflatable protective deployment (novelty pressure, lower direct-infringement risk).** A dense field of inflatable devices that deploy on a sensed event, but for *different use cases* — cushioning the wearer's own fall, or cushioning a collision with a vehicle/robot — not interposing a barrier to protect a third party from an environmental hazard. These mostly threaten *patentability/novelty* of a broadly-worded inflation claim, not FTO, because their claims are tied to their specific use.

**Tier 3 — Barriers, guarding, robot confinement, child gates (background; low direct risk).** Static guarding, wall-mounted child gates, and virtual-barrier art that restricts *the robot's own* movement. Shows the space is crowded but is not directly on-point.

## 1.5 Infringement-risk analysis of the Tier-1 references

### US 11,331,805 B2 (Sony) — the controlling reference

Independent claim 1 (paraphrased) requires, in essence: a robot with one or more **barrier elements**; a processing unit that **identifies boundaries** = allowed extent of user motion; a control unit that **positions the robot or a barrier element as a safety barrier**; and specifically **identifies the closest point to the user and positions the robot at the periphery of a "safe space" indicating the allowed extent of user motion before the user encounters a hazard.** Dependent claims add: robot positioned **between the user and the closest boundary** (cl. 6) or **between the user and the boundary the user is moving toward** (cl. 7).

Element-by-element read against the concept:

- "robot comprising barrier element(s)" → an inflatable barrier carried by the robot **is** a barrier element. *Reads on.*
- "positions the robot or a barrier element to act as a safety barrier" between user and hazard/boundary → the concept does exactly this. *Reads on (cl. 6/7).*
- **Inflatability is NOT a limitation** of claim 1. So making the barrier inflatable does **not, by itself, avoid** claim 1. This is the key FTO trap.

**Two things narrow claim 1, and are the seeds of a design-around:**
1. Claim 1's core limitation is framed around a **"safe space" defining the allowed extent of the *user's own* motion** — i.e., keeping *the user* inside a bounded region (consistent with the Sony/PlayStation "play space / guardian" origin). A product whose purpose and control logic is to **block a vulnerable third party (child/pet) from a specific external hazard** — rather than to define/enforce the *user's* permitted play area — may fall outside the precise wording of claim 1, though dependent claims 6/7 ("between the user and the boundary") are closer and riskier.
2. Claim 1 does not recite deployment **beyond the robot's footprint by inflation from a stowed state**, a standardized quick-change coupling, or fail-safe deflation.

**Verdict on US 11,331,805:** genuine, non-trivial infringement risk for any product that "positions a barrier between a person and a hazard." Inflation alone is not a design-around. Must obtain: (i) the **family / legal status in EP and CN** (it is a US grant assigned to Sony Interactive Entertainment; whether counterparts are pending/granted/in-force in your target markets is decisive and unknown here); (ii) a **claim-construction + validity** view from counsel (is claim 1 valid over earlier robot-guarding and confinement art? a validity weakness would reduce the risk).

### US 10,943,456 (IBM "Virtual safety guardian")

Threatens a **broad method claim** of the form "detect that a child is near a hazard and deploy a protective device." Its disclosure expressly covers children, water, and fire, and "activating protective devices" / "deploying flotation devices" from an intervention library. This is why the NEURA claims must **not** be drafted at that level of generality. It is less of a direct *apparatus* infringement risk (it reads as a detection/decision system selecting from a library of actions), but it is strong prior art against — and a potential infringement risk for — an over-broad "sense-and-deploy-protection" method.

## 1.6 Design-around opportunities

The defensible whitespace, and the design-arounds that both **reduce infringement exposure** and **carry the novelty** for NEURA's own patent:

1. **Inflatable, stowed→deployed geometry that exceeds the robot footprint.** Neither Tier-1 reference discloses a barrier that is *inflated from a compact stored volume* to a much larger compliant boundary. This is a concrete structural/functional difference to build claims on.
2. **Modular quick-change end-effector.** The barrier as a *swappable* module on a standardized mechanical/electrical/data interface (ties to your VIS/VMM work) is absent from the Tier-1 art. This both differentiates and aligns with your platform strategy.
3. **Soft, impact-attenuating compliance with a specified energy-absorption function.** Frame the barrier not merely as an obstacle but as an *impact-attenuating* compliant surface (a technical effect that also helps EPO/CNIPA patentability). Sony's barrier elements are described as rigid/rotatable panels.
4. **Fail-safe behavior** (controlled deflation/retraction to a defined safe state on fault or power loss) — absent from Tier-1, and valuable for certification.
5. **Function framing to skirt Sony claim 1:** design the control logic around *interposing between a subject and an identified external hazard object/edge*, not around *enforcing the allowed extent of the user's own motion within a safe space*. (Note dependents 6/7 remain a risk — counsel should assess.)
6. **Retraction/reusability** and **multi-cell selectable geometry** (wall vs. ring vs. canopy) — implementation detail that is novel and useful as dependent-claim fallback.

## 1.7 Overall FTO assessment

**Constrained but workable — conditional on a family/validity check.** The **broad concept** ("a robot interposes a protective barrier between a person and a hazard") is **not clear** — US 11,331,805 (Sony) is a real obstacle, and inflatability does not avoid it. Whether this actually blocks NEURA in Europe and China depends entirely on the **legal status and claim scope of the Sony family in those jurisdictions**, which must be checked first. The **specific implementation** (inflatable, modular quick-change, footprint-exceeding, soft/fail-safe) appears **patentable** for NEURA and provides the basis for design-arounds, but a NEURA patent on the improvement would **not** by itself grant freedom to operate against a dominant Sony claim.

Recommended risk-mitigation sequence: (1) professional family/legal-status + claim-scope search on US 11,331,805 and US 10,943,456 (EP/CN counterparts); (2) validity assessment of Sony claim 1 over earlier guarding/confinement art; (3) if the dominant claim is in force and valid in your markets, evaluate design-around (§1.6) vs. license vs. avoid; (4) file NEURA's own narrow application (Part 2) regardless, to protect the specific improvement and create cross-licensing leverage; (5) because this is a **life-safety device**, budget for product-liability and regulatory review (ISO 13482 for personal-care robots; machinery/GPSR framework in the EU) — this raises the bar but also deepens the moat.

# PART 2 — PATENT APPLICATION DRAFT

> Draft for attorney review. Bracketed items **[like this]** need engineering values or a drawing. The claims are written to emphasize the features that distinguish over US 11,331,805 and US 10,943,456.

## 2.1 Title of the Invention

**Robotic End-Effector and Method for Interposing a Deployable Inflatable Compliant Barrier Between a Protected Subject and an Environmental Hazard**

*(Alternative shorter titles for filing: "Deployable Inflatable Safety-Barrier End-Effector for a Robot"; "Robotic Inflatable Barrier for Hazard Interposition.")*

## 2.2 Technical Field

The present disclosure relates to robotic safety systems, and more particularly to a modular, robot-mountable end-effector comprising a deployable inflatable barrier, and to methods of controlling a robot to interpose such a barrier between a protected subject and an environmental hazard.

## 2.3 Background

Home and service robots increasingly operate in unstructured human environments containing hazards to vulnerable subjects such as young children and pets — for example open staircases and elevation drop-offs, bodies of water, hot cooking surfaces, roadways, and sharp or powered implements. Existing safety measures are inadequate for a mobile robot acting as an active guardian:

- **Static barriers and child gates** must be pre-installed at fixed locations, cannot adapt to a moving subject or a transient hazard, and provide no protection away from the mounting point.
- **Wearable fall-protection airbags** require the protected person to wear the device and only cushion that person's own fall; they do not interpose a boundary and are impractical for children and pets and for hazards other than falls.
- **Collision airbags** for vehicles and collaborative robots are directed to cushioning an impact with the machine itself or an occupant, not to protecting a third party from an *environmental* hazard.
- **Robots that position their own body as a barrier** are limited by the robot's physical footprint and mass, cannot rapidly extend the protected span, and require the robot to physically occupy the entire boundary.
- **Guardian systems that select an intervention from a library** address detection and decision-making but do not provide a specific, rapidly-deployable physical barrier mechanism suitable as a modular robot end-effector.

There is a need for a robot-carried device that can rapidly form a soft, compliant physical boundary — larger than the robot's own footprint — between a moving vulnerable subject and a variable environmental hazard, that can be attached to a robot as an interchangeable module, and that behaves in a fail-safe manner.

## 2.4 Summary of the Invention

In one aspect, an **end-effector** for a robot comprises a coupling for releasably mounting the end-effector to a complementary interface of a robot; an **inflatable barrier member** movable between a stowed configuration (first volume) and a deployed configuration (second, larger volume) in which it extends **beyond a footprint of the robot** to define a **compliant boundary surface**; an **inflation source** operable to transition the barrier member to the deployed configuration in response to a deployment signal; and a **controller** that generates the deployment signal in response to sensor data indicating that a **protected subject, distinct from the robot,** is within a predetermined proximity of an **environmental hazard**, such that the deployed barrier member interposes the compliant boundary surface between the protected subject and the environmental hazard.

In another aspect, a **method** comprises determining from sensor data that a protected subject is approaching or within a predetermined proximity of an environmental hazard; causing a robot to position an inflatable barrier member carried by the robot between the subject and the hazard; and **inflating** the barrier member from a stowed to a deployed configuration in which it forms a compliant boundary extending beyond the robot's footprint and physically impeding movement of the subject toward the hazard.

## 2.6 Detailed Description with Embodiments

**Robot and end-effector.** Referring to FIGS. 1–2, a robot 100 (e.g., a mobile manipulator with a wheeled/tracked base and one or more arms, or a stationary arm) carries an end-effector 200 via a coupling 210. The robot 100 has a footprint F defined by its ground-contact or body envelope. The end-effector 200 includes an inflatable barrier member 220, an inflation source 230, a controller 240, and one or more sensors 250 (which may be wholly or partly on the robot 100). In the stowed configuration (FIG. 1) the barrier member 220 has a first volume V1 and is compactly folded/rolled/packed within or against a housing 222. In the deployed configuration (FIG. 2) the barrier member 220 has a second volume V2 > V1 and extends a span S beyond the footprint F.

**Coupling (FIG. 3).** The coupling 210 releasably mounts the end-effector to a complementary interface of the robot and, in embodiments, comprises a standardized **mechanical interface** 211 (e.g., bayonet, threaded ring, or lever-cam quick-connect with self-alignment and multiple discrete rotational indexing positions), an **electrical interface** 212 (e.g., dual power rails such as [48 V] and [24 V], and a hot-swap-capable pin arrangement), and a **data interface** 213 (e.g., a real-time bus such as CAN-FD and/or a higher-bandwidth link such as Ethernet). In embodiments the end-effector stores and transmits, over the data interface, a **machine-readable capability manifest** declaring at least a barrier-deployment capability and one or more safety constraints (e.g., maximum inflation pressure, deployment envelope, deflation time), enabling the robot to admit and configure the module. This allows the end-effector to be one of a plurality of interchangeable modules of a platform.

**Barrier member.** The barrier member 220 comprises one or more inflatable chambers formed of a flexible, optionally fabric-reinforced, gas-retaining material. A surface 224 of the deployed member presents a **compliant boundary** configured to **attenuate impact energy** of a subject contacting it — e.g., by controlled compliance of the inflated chamber and/or by a pressure-relief element 226 that vents above a threshold contact force to limit peak force on the subject. In embodiments the member 220 comprises a plurality of independently inflatable cells 228 (FIG. 8), enabling selectable deployed geometries: a planar wall (FIG. 7A) for an edge/drop-off, a ring or partial enclosure (FIG. 7B) around a subject or hazard, or an overhead canopy (FIG. 7C) for a falling-object or heat hazard.

**Inflation source.** The inflation source 230 may comprise a stored compressed-gas cartridge and valve, a pyrotechnic or chemical gas generator, an electrically driven blower/pump, or a combination (e.g., a fast gas generator for initial deployment and a pump for sustained pressure and re-inflation). In embodiments the inflation source transitions the member 220 from stowed to deployed in less than **[1 second]** (or **[500 ms]**) from the deployment signal.

**Sensing and control (FIG. 4).** Sensors 250 may include one or more cameras, depth/ToF sensors, proximity/radar/ultrasonic sensors, and/or thermal sensors. The controller 240 processes sensor data to: (i) detect and classify a **protected subject** (e.g., a subject classified as a child or a pet, distinguished from an adult operator); (ii) detect and classify an **environmental hazard** (e.g., an edge/drop-off, a body of water, a hot surface above a temperature threshold, a roadway, a powered or sharp implement); and (iii) determine that the subject is approaching or within a predetermined proximity of the hazard, optionally using a predicted trajectory of the subject. On such determination the controller generates the **deployment signal**.

**Deployment and interposition (FIGS. 5–6, 9).** In response, the robot 100 positions the end-effector such that, when deployed, the compliant boundary surface 224 is **interposed between the subject and the hazard**; where the robot is mobile, the controller first causes navigation to an interposition pose. The inflation source then deploys the member 220. In embodiments the robot maintains position and/or braces the barrier (e.g., against the floor or a wall) to react loads from the subject contacting the barrier.

**Fail-safe behavior.** The controller 240 is configured, on detection of a fault condition, loss of power, or clearance of the hazard, to place the barrier member in a **predetermined safe state**, e.g., controlled deflation and retraction to the stowed configuration, or maintaining inflation until a safe hand-off. A watchdog and a normally-safe valve arrangement ensure the safe state is reached without dependence on higher-level software.

**Further embodiments.** The barrier surface may include a visual and/or auditory alert (e.g., illumination or an alarm) to warn the subject or summon a caregiver. The end-effector may be used by a stationary robot arm mounted near a fixed hazard (e.g., a kitchen island) as well as by a mobile robot. The barrier may be retracted and re-deployed multiple times. The protected subject need not wear any device.

## 2.8 Abstract

An end-effector for a robot includes a coupling for releasably mounting the end-effector to a robot, an inflatable barrier member movable between a compact stowed configuration and a deployed configuration in which it extends beyond the robot's footprint to define a soft compliant boundary surface, an inflation source that deploys the barrier member in response to a deployment signal, and a controller that generates the signal when sensor data indicates a protected subject — distinct from the robot, such as a child or pet — is near an environmental hazard such as a drop-off, water, hot surface, or roadway. The deployed barrier is interposed between the subject and the hazard and, in embodiments, attenuates impact energy, adopts a selectable geometry, and deflates in a fail-safe manner. Related methods and a robot incorporating the end-effector are disclosed.

## 3.1 How the claims maximize patentability and minimize infringement risk

- **Distinguish structurally over Sony US 11,331,805 in the independent claim.** The independent claim recites three features absent from Sony's granted claim 1: (a) an **inflatable barrier member movable stowed→deployed by inflation**; (b) **extends beyond the footprint of the robot**; (c) a **releasable coupling** (modular end-effector). Keeping all three in claim 1 both supports novelty/inventive step and steers the *product* away from the plain reading of Sony's positioning claim. (Caution: Sony dependents 6/7 still describe positioning "between the user and the boundary"; counsel must confirm the interposition language does not read onto them.)
- **Do not draft any independent claim at the IBM US 10,943,456 level** ("detect hazard → deploy protective device"). Every independent claim ties the intervention to the **specific inflatable, footprint-exceeding, compliant barrier mechanism**. This avoids the guardian-library prior art.
- **Layer fallback positions in the dependents:** impact attenuation + pressure relief (cl. 2–3), deployment speed (cl. 5, 22), fail-safe deflation (cl. 6–7, 18), standardized interface + capability manifest (cl. 8–9, 21) — each is an independently novel narrowing that can survive if a broader claim falls.
- **EPO framing (technical character / COMVIK):** emphasize the *physical technical effects* — rapid formation of an energy-absorbing boundary of controlled geometry from a compact module, and fail-safe kinematics. Avoid framing the "guardian" purpose as the inventive contribution; the mechanism is the contribution. The subject-classification (child/pet) and hazard-classification steps are supporting, not the sole basis of inventive step.
- **CNIPA framing:** present as a technical solution solving the technical problem of forming, from a compact robot-mounted module, a compliant protective boundary larger than the robot within a bounded time; file the four-part suite (method claim 16, apparatus claim 1, robot/device claim 23, storage-medium claim 24), which matches Chinese practice.
- **Emphasize the non-obvious combination:** inflatable-airbag art teaches inflation for *cushioning the wearer's/occupant's own impact*; barrier-positioning art teaches *rigid* barrier placement to bound the *user's own* motion. Combining them to *interpose an inflatable, footprint-exceeding, energy-absorbing barrier to protect a distinct third party from an external hazard* is the argued inventive step; the prior art teaches away (wearable = on the protected person; collision airbag = protect from the machine, not the environment).
